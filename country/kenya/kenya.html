Kenya and its wildlife !

Kenya, in long form the Republic of Kenya, is a country in East Africa, bordering the Indian Ocean and bordering Tanzania to the south, Uganda to the west, South Sudan to the northwest, Ethiopia to the north and Somalia to the east. It is also bisected, almost equally, by the Earth's equator Its capital is Nairobi and it has a population of approximately 47,500,000

climate

Kenya's climate is very diverse. The Lake Victoria Basin and the southern coastline have a warm, humid tropical climate, according to Köppen's classification, while the northern coastline has a tropical savannah climate. The central region has a cold, wet, montane climate and the eastern and northern regions have a hot, dry, semi-arid climate. The driest periods are from December to March and from May to October. The winter extends from July to September, which are rather gray months. The lowest average temperatures are between 17 and 19 °C, the highest between 27 and 29 °C. The lowest night-time temperatures are recorded in August and September with temperatures around 12°C. Daytime highs can reach 37°C between December and April. There are two rainy seasons, the most abundant occurs between March and May. The highest rainfall occurs in April, with 200 mm. A small rainy season also occurs in November and December with an average of 100 mm and a maximum of 140 mm. The driest month, with an average of between 8 and 79 mm, is January

Population

The Great Rift Valley is often referred to as the "cradle of humanity" because of the many hominid fossils that have been found there. The oldest, Miocene Proconsul, was discovered on the volcanic island of Rusinga by paleo-primate and archaeologist Louis Leakey. Other findings indicate that hominids such as Homo habilis and Homo ergaster lived in present-day Kenya 2.5 million years ago. People from the northeast, speaking a Cushitic language, arrived in the coastal region around 2000 BC. In the transition from the 2nd to the 1st millennium B.C., the first Bantu people arrived from the west, the so-called "Urewe culture", named after the archaeological site in central Nyanza where it was first discovered. The arrival of other Bantu peoples continued until the end of the first millennium A.D. Finally, from 1500 A.D. onwards, the Nilotic-speaking peoples arrived from the northwest and more specifically from the Nile Valley, hence their name Nilote. Currently, Kenya has 70 tribal groups from these three different ethnic groups. The largest group is of Bantu origin, including the Kikuyu (22%), the Luhya (14%), the Kisii (6%), the Meru (6%) and the Embus. Almost equally divided are the Nilotic group such as the Luo (14%), Kalenjin (12%), Maasai, Samburu, Pokot and Turkana. This is followed by the Cushitic group such as the Orma, Somali and Borana.

Parks and nature reserves

Kenya's major tourist attraction is its national parks and reserves, which account for 8% of the country's total area, or 46,430 km2 . Managed by the Kenya Wildlife Service (KWS), they consist of 26 parks, of which 22 are terrestrial and 4 are marine, 34 reserves, of which 28 are terrestrial and 6 are marine, as well as 5 sanctuaries.
